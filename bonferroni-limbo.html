The Bonferroni limbo

Why more comparisons means a stricter threshold — and how Bonferroni adjustment works

How to use: Use the slider to set the number of groups, then click "Run the comparisons" to simulate the limbo.

The bar represents the Bonferroni-adjusted alpha level (.05 divided by the number of comparisons). As you increase the number of groups, more comparisons are needed, and the bar drops lower — making it harder for any comparison to pass.

The figures represent the p-values from each pairwise comparison. A figure's height corresponds to its p-value — a taller figure has a higher p-value, and a shorter figure has a lower p-value.

The result: If a figure is short enough to make it under the bar, that comparison is statistically significant at the Bonferroni-adjusted alpha level. If a figure is too tall to pass under the bar, that comparison is not statistically significant at the adjusted level — even if it would have been significant at the original .05.
