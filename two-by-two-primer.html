How do researchers turn a 2x2 table into epidemiological values?

Every epidemiological statistic you'll encounter in a journal article — RR, ARR, NNT, OR — comes from the same simple table. Click a statistic below to see which cells it uses, then try changing the numbers.

How to use this tool: Read the scenario, then click any of the four statistic cards below the table. The cells used in that calculation will light up, and the formula and result will appear. You can also edit the numbers in the table to see how each statistic changes.
÷Relative Risk (RR)▼
The relative risk compares the rate of an outcome in one group to the rate in the other. Think of it as: how many times more (or less) likely is this outcome in Group 1?
How to read it:RR = 1 — No difference between groupsRR > 1 — Higher rate in Group 1RR < 1 — Lower rate in Group 1
↔ARR▼
The actual gap in rates between the two groups, measured in percentage points. A "2x increase" could mean 1% vs. 2% or 30% vs. 60% — the ARR tells you which.
How to read it:ARR = 0% — No difference between groupsLarger ARR — Bigger real-world gapSmaller ARR — Smaller gap (even if RR looks big)
👤NNT / NNH▼
How many patients must receive the treatment for one additional patient to benefit (NNT)? Or how many must be exposed for one additional person to be harmed (NNH)?
How to read it:Smaller NNT — Bigger per-person impactLarger NNT — Many patients needed per benefit
×Odds Ratio (OR)▼
The odds ratio compares the odds of an outcome in one group to the odds in another. It uses the diagonal cells of the 2x2 table — not the row totals.
How to read it:OR = 1 — No difference between groupsOR > 1 — Higher odds in Group 1OR < 1 — Lower odds in Group 1
A large cohort study followed 1,000 adults over 20 years to determine whether smoking is associated with developing lung cancer. Half of the participants were smokers and half were non-smokers at baseline.
Below depicts the 2x2 table for this study:
The two groups (rows): Smokers and Non-smokers
The two outcomes (columns): Developed Lung Cancer and No Lung Cancer
For this study, researchers defined:
Developed Lung Cancer = confirmed diagnosis during the 20-year follow-up
No Lung Cancer = no diagnosis during the follow-up period
