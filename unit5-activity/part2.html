2
What does Relative Risk actually compare?

Relative Risk is a ratio of two risks. It compares the risk in one group to the risk in another. The number always sits somewhere on a scale anchored at 1.0 — the point of "no difference."

Below is the same RR scale you'll see on the left panel later. Drag the handle or use the buttons to land on different RR values. Watch what each value means.

Notice the rule:

• Slide left of 1 → treatment reduces risk (protective)
• Sit on 1 → both groups have equal risk (no effect)
• Slide right of 1 → treatment is associated with more risk (harmful direction)

The further from 1, the bigger the effect. RR = 1.0 is the only value where the two groups behave identically.

★ So what does 0.45 mean?

Young adults who wore custom earplugs were about 0.45 times as likely — or roughly 55% less likely — to develop measurable hearing loss compared with peers who wore no protection. That's a meaningful protective effect, and the CI tells you it's not just chance.

5
Four rules to carry into any paper

Whenever you encounter an RR in published research, these four ideas will get you to a confident interpretation:

• RR = 1.00 → both groups have equal risk; the exposure or treatment makes no difference
• RR > 1.00 → outcome is more likely in the treatment/exposed group
• RR < 1.00 → outcome is less likely in the treatment/exposed group
• CI excluding 1.00 → the result is statistically significant

Hold these in mind. The next step lets you change the article's data and see RR move in real time.

Big takeaways

What RR will tell you in any paper

  • RR is a ratio of two risks — risk in the treatment/exposed group divided by risk in the comparison group. It is not a count of patients and not a percentage point difference.
  • RR = 1.0 means no difference. When both groups have the same risk, the ratio is exactly 1. This is the "null value" — and it's why every CI you read should be checked against 1.0.
  • The further from 1.0, the bigger the effect. RR < 1 means the exposure/treatment is protective; RR > 1 means it raises risk. The numbers are not symmetric — RR = 0.5 (halves risk) and RR = 2.0 (doubles risk) describe the same magnitude in opposite directions.
  • A 95% CI that excludes 1.0 = statistically significant. A tight CI like [0.40, 0.55] means a sharp estimate; a wide CI like [0.20, 0.95] means more uncertainty.
  • RR doesn't tell you absolute counts. For "how many patients are actually spared," you need Absolute Risk Reduction — coming up next in Part 3.
