Orientation

In Part 2 you saw that RR doesn't tell you how many patients are actually spared. Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR) answers that question. It's a subtraction, not a ratio — and it's often what matters most for clinical decisions. Let's walk through it.

CI [18%, 42%]The interval excludes 0, so the reduction is statistically significant. (For ARR, the null value is 0, not 1.)

ARR is the gap between two risks

While RR is a ratio of two risks, ARR is the difference. It tells you, in plain percentage points, how much smaller the treatment group's risk is.

Three meaningful zones for ARR. Positive ARR (gap pointing toward less risk in the treatment group) means the treatment is protective. ARR = 0 means both groups have the same risk — the treatment makes no difference. Negative ARR means the treatment group fared worse than the comparison — the treatment was harmful.

Notice that ARR is in percentage points. A reduction from 55% to 25% is a 30 percentage point drop, not a 30% drop in relative terms. This distinction trips up readers and even authors.

★ So what does ARR = 30 pp mean?

For every 100 young adults fitted with custom earplugs, about 30 fewer will develop measurable hearing loss compared with 100 going unprotected. That's a number patients can hold onto: 30 fewer per 100. ARR is what makes RR concrete.

5
Four rules to remember when reading any ARR

ARR shows up in clinical literature constantly — sometimes labeled "absolute risk difference" or "risk difference." These four rules carry through:

• ARR = 0 → no difference between the groups; the null value for ARR
• ARR > 0 → treatment group has lower risk (protective)
• ARR < 0 → treatment group has higher risk (harmful)
• CI excluding 0 → the difference is statistically significant

Notice the null value for ARR is 0 — different from RR's null of 1. Why? Because ARR is a difference: when groups are identical, the difference is zero. When you read CIs for ARR, check whether they cross 0 (not 1).

Also: ARR is reported in percentage points, not percent. A 30 percentage-point reduction from 55% is much bigger than a "30% reduction" sounds at first. The mock article's CI [18%, 42%] is in percentage points too.

Big takeaways

What ARR will tell you in any paper

  • ARR is the difference between two risks — risk in the comparison group minus risk in the treatment group. It's a subtraction, not a ratio.
  • ARR's null value is 0, not 1. Both groups identical → difference is zero. When you read CIs for ARR, check whether they cross 0.
  • ARR is in percentage points, not percent. A drop from 55% to 25% is 30 percentage points. Saying "30%" without "points" is the most common reporting error.
  • ARR makes RR concrete. Two studies can have identical RR but very different ARR. ARR is what tells the patient: "X fewer per 100 — this is what the treatment actually buys you."
  • ARR is the bridge to NNT. If ARR is 30 percentage points (0.30 as a proportion), then 1/0.30 ≈ 3 to 4 — and that's how many patients you'd need to fit with earplugs to prevent one case of hearing loss. That's Number Needed to Treat, coming up in Part 4.
