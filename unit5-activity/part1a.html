Reading epidemiology like a clinician

Every clinical journal you'll read in OT or audiology uses the language of epidemiology — risk ratios, odds ratios, absolute risk reduction, number needed to treat. These four numbers tell you how much an intervention helps, how much an exposure harms, and how often you'll actually see it in your caseload.

This activity walks you through all four — one at a time, anchored to a single mock article, with interactive practice. By the end, you'll be able to read any epidemiology paper with confidence and translate the findings to a patient or colleague.

What is epidemiology, and why does an OT or AuD care?

Epidemiology is the study of how often health conditions occur in groups of people, and what factors influence those rates. For clinicians, it's the bridge between research and practice: how much does an intervention actually help? How dangerous is a particular exposure? Will the effect be big enough to matter for the patient sitting in front of you?

If you've ever read a study and felt unsure whether a "significant" finding was actually clinically meaningful — that's epidemiological literacy. It's a learnable skill, and it's the focus of this activity.

The four values you'll learn to read

Every epidemiology paper that compares groups (a treatment vs. control, or an exposure vs. no exposure) reports some combination of these four statistics. You'll meet each one in detail in Parts 2–5.

RR
Relative Risk
A ratio comparing the risk of an outcome between two groups. RR = 1 means equal risk; RR < 1 means lower risk in the exposed/treated group; RR > 1 means higher.
▸ You'll meet this in Part 2
ARR / ARI
Absolute Risk Reduction (or Increase)
The actual percentage-point difference in risk between two groups. RR tells you the ratio; ARR tells you the magnitude in real terms.
▸ You'll meet this in Part 3
NNT / NNH
Number Needed to Treat (or Harm)
How many patients need to be treated (or exposed) for one extra outcome — beneficial or harmful. The most patient-friendly statistic of the four.
▸ You'll meet this in Part 4
OR
Odds Ratio
A ratio of odds (not risks) between two groups. Looks like RR but uses different math. Common in case-control studies and logistic regression.
▸ You'll meet this in Part 5

A quick note on prevalence

You'll occasionally see prevalence reported in epi papers — it's the proportion of people in a population who have a condition at a given time (e.g., "the prevalence of presbycusis among adults over 70 is approximately 50%"). It tells you how common something is, but not whether one group is at higher risk than another.

In this activity

We won't focus on prevalence calculations. Recognize the term when you see it, and know that prevalence describes how common a condition is, while RR, ARR, NNT, and OR all describe how exposure or treatment changes risk between groups. Different questions, different statistics.
