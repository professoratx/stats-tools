Orientation

You've now seen RR (a ratio of risks), ARR (the gap between risks), and NNT (clinical efficiency). The fourth value, the Odds Ratio (OR), is closely related to RR but uses a different denominator: it's a ratio of odds, not risks. OR shows up everywhere — case-control studies, logistic regression, meta-analyses. And it has one important quirk worth understanding before you read another paper.

The claimNotice the language: "lower odds" — not "lower risk." That's the giveaway you're reading an OR. Authors often slip into "lower risk" language when they mean odds, but careful writers (and journals) preserve the distinction.
OR = 0.27OR < 1 means lower odds in the treatment group. OR = 1 is the null. OR > 1 means higher odds. Note: this OR (0.27) is further from 1 than the RR (0.45) you saw in Part 2 — for the same data. That's not a mistake; it's the OR's quirk.
CI [0.15, 0.50]The interval excludes 1, so the result is statistically significant. Just like RR, the null value for OR is 1 (not 0). The interval is also asymmetric on the linear scale — wider on the larger side. That's because OR is interpreted on a log scale.
2
Odds ≠ risk — and they only match when the outcome is rare

Risk is the proportion of patients with the outcome out of everyone. Odds is the ratio of patients with the outcome to those without. They sound similar, but they behave differently.

When outcomes are rare (≤10%), risk and odds are nearly identical.

Notice the pattern: as the outcome becomes more common, odds inflate while risk stays bounded between 0 and 1. At 50% affected, risk = 0.50 but odds = 1.0. At 80% affected, risk = 0.80 but odds = 4.0.

This is why the OR diverges from the RR as outcomes become more common — and why OR can exaggerate the apparent effect size compared with RR.

With these article values (outcome 25–55%), OR (0.27) is meaningfully different from RR (0.45) — OR exaggerates the effect.

The pattern: when the outcome is rare (<10%), OR and RR are nearly identical — most papers in epidemiology use this approximation safely. When the outcome is common (>10%), OR drifts further from 1 than RR, making the effect look bigger than it really is. This is critical to know when reading any paper that reports OR for a common outcome.

The fast way to compute OR uses the diagonal cross-product of the 2×2 table. Click the buttons below in order to walk through it — each button highlights the relevant cells in the contingency table on the left panel.

OR = (a × d) / (b × c)
OR = (25 × 45) / (75 × 55) = 1125 / 4125 = 0.27

Where a, b, c, d are the four cells of the 2×2 table:
a = earplugs with hearing loss (25)
b = earplugs without hearing loss (75)
c = no protection with hearing loss (55)

★ So what does OR = 0.27 mean?

The odds of measurable hearing loss in earplug-fitted patients are about 27% of the odds in unprotected patients — a substantial reduction. But because the outcome here is common (25–55%), this OR overstates the effect compared with RR. RR = 0.45 (a 55% relative risk reduction) is the more accurate description for clinical communication.

5
Four rules to remember when reading any OR

OR shows up everywhere in clinical and public-health literature. These four rules will guide your reading:

• Null value is 1 — same as RR. CI excluding 1 = statistically significant.
• OR < 1 = protective; OR > 1 = risk factor — same direction logic as RR.
• OR ≈ RR only when outcome is rare (<10%). When the outcome is common, OR moves further from 1 than RR — translating "OR" as "risk" overstates the effect.
• Where you see it: case-control studies (where you can't compute RR directly), logistic regression coefficients, meta-analyses, and adjusted analyses controlling for covariates.

Why is OR used at all if it's confusing? Because in case-control studies, the researchers select subjects based on outcome status, making true risk impossible to compute. OR is the only valid effect estimate for that design. And in logistic regression — the workhorse model for binary outcomes — OR comes out naturally as the exponentiated coefficient.

Big takeaways

What OR will tell you in any paper

  • OR is a ratio of odds, not risks. Odds = affected ÷ unaffected (a:b), not affected ÷ total. The two diverge as the outcome becomes more common.
  • Null value is 1, just like RR. OR < 1 = protective. OR > 1 = risk factor. CI excluding 1 = statistically significant.
  • OR ≈ RR only when outcome is rare (< 10%). When outcomes are common, OR exaggerates the effect — moving further from 1 than the corresponding RR. Translating "OR" as "risk" in a common-outcome study overstates the finding.
  • OR is the cross-product (a × d) ÷ (b × c). A fast trick: opposite corners of the 2×2 table multiplied together.
  • Where you'll see OR: case-control studies (where RR can't be computed), logistic regression (the natural output), adjusted analyses controlling for confounders, and meta-analyses.
