Apply what you've learned

You've worked through RR, ARR, NNT, and OR using a protective intervention (custom earplugs prevent hearing loss). Now flip the script: you're reading a study where the exposure increases risk. The math is identical — but the language changes. RR > 1 instead of RR < 1. ARI (Absolute Risk Increase) instead of ARR. NNH (Number Needed to Harm) instead of NNT. The study below tracked office workers over 12 months. Your job: read it like a clinician.

1
The article
From the Results section

Sustained Computer Use and 12-Month Incidence of Hand/Wrist Repetitive Strain Injury in Office Workers

A 12-month prospective cohort study (n = 400)

Among office workers reporting ≥6 hours/day of sustained computer use, 60 of 200 (30.0%) developed clinically diagnosed hand or wrist RSI during the 12-month follow-up period. Among workers reporting <3 hours/day of sustained computer use, 24 of 200 (12.0%) developed RSI.

Compared with the low-exposure group, high-exposure workers had a markedly increased risk of RSI (RR = 2.50, 95% CI [1.62, 3.86], p < .001). The absolute risk increase was 18 percentage points (95% CI [10%, 26%]); the corresponding number needed to harm was 6 (95% CI [4, 10]). The odds ratio comparing high to low exposure was 3.14 (95% CI [1.85, 5.34]).

In a planned secondary analysis, regular use of an anti-fatigue mat at the workstation showed a suggestive but non-significant protective association with RSI (RR = 0.78, 95% CI [0.55, 1.11]).

Take a moment to read the excerpt and check the reference card on the left. Notice the framing language — "increased risk," "harm," "compared with the low-exposure group." That's how risk-factor studies talk.

Big takeaways

Reading risk-factor studies — five things to remember

  • The math doesn't change — just the language. RR, ARI, NNH, and OR for risk factors use identical formulas to RR, ARR, NNT, and OR for protective interventions. What changes is whether the ratio is <1 or >1, and whether you call the difference a reduction or an increase.
  • RR > 1 means percent increase = (RR − 1) × 100, not RR × 100. RR = 2.50 is a 150% increase in risk, not 250%. This is the most common misreading you'll encounter in workplace health communications and patient-facing materials.
  • NNH is the harm-side mirror of NNT. Same formula (1 / |risk difference|), same interpretation rules — just describing extra cases of harm rather than extra cases prevented. A small NNH means strong harm; a small NNT means strong benefit.
  • Non-significant ≠ no effect, and non-significant ≠ proven safe. When a CI crosses 1 (for ratios) or 0 (for differences), the data don't let you rule out the null. The point estimate may still be the best guess, but you can't claim significance — and you can't claim the intervention "doesn't work" either. Often the right read is: "results are inconclusive; a larger study could clarify."
  • Statistical findings inform — they don't dictate — clinical decisions. A statistically significant elevated risk tells you something matters at the population level. Translating that into a recommendation for one patient still requires clinical judgment: their baseline risk, their context, their preferences, and the available alternatives.
