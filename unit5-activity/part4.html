Orientation

In Part 3 you saw that ARR tells you how many fewer patients per 100 will have the outcome. NNT takes that number and flips it on its head — telling you how many patients you'd need to treat to prevent one case. It's the most directly clinical of the four numbers, and the one your patients will most easily understand.

CI [3, 6]The CI for NNT is also reported as whole numbers. Plausibly, the true number you'd need to fit ranges from 3 to 6. Smaller is better — fewer patients fitted to prevent one case = more efficient intervention.
2
NNT is the inverse of ARR

Here's the link: NNT = 1 ÷ ARR (where ARR is in proportion form, not percentage points). The smaller the ARR, the larger the NNT — you need to treat more patients to prevent one case. Drag the slider to see this relationship in action.

If ARR = 30 pp (or 0.30 as a proportion)
You'd need to treat 4 patients to prevent 1 case
ARR (pp) 30

Notice the pattern: smaller NNT = stronger intervention. NNT = 2 means you only need to treat 2 patients to spare 1 case — that's a powerful intervention. NNT = 50 means you'd need to treat 50 patients to spare 1 — still real, but each individual patient has a much smaller chance of personally benefiting.

And as ARR approaches 0, NNT shoots toward infinity — when there's no real effect, no number of patients treated will reliably prevent the outcome.

4
Compute and round, then click the card

The math is one division and a rounding step.

NNT = 1 / ARR (as proportion)
NNT = 1 / 0.30 = 3.33 → round up to 4|

NNT is always rounded up, never down. You can't fit 3.33 patients with earplugs, and rounding down would understate how many you actually need.

★ So what does NNT = 4 mean?

For every 4 young adults fitted with custom earplugs, one fewer case of measurable hearing loss will occur over 2 years. That's a number you can use in counseling — concrete, actionable, and tied to a specific timeframe and outcome.

5
Four rules to remember when reading any NNT

NNT shows up in clinical guidelines, treatment recommendations, and conversations with patients. These four rules apply across all of them:

• Smaller NNT = stronger effect. NNT = 2 is a powerhouse; NNT = 100 is borderline.
• Always round up. NNT = 3.33 rounds to 4, not 3 — never understate how many you need to treat.
• NNT depends on baseline risk. A treatment with the same RR can have very different NNT in different populations.
• Always paired with a timeframe and outcome. "NNT = 4 over 24 months to prevent measurable hearing loss" — strip those details and the number becomes meaningless.

Also: when the intervention causes harm, the related number is NNH (Number Needed to Harm). Same math, opposite direction. You'll see it in any paper that reports adverse events.

Big takeaways

What NNT will tell you in any paper

  • NNT is the inverse of ARR. NNT = 1/ARR (with ARR as a proportion). It tells you how many patients you'd need to treat to prevent one case of the outcome.
  • Smaller NNT = stronger intervention. NNT = 2 is exceptional; NNT = 100 is a weak effect, even if statistically significant.
  • Always round up. NNT = 3.33 rounds to 4. You can't fit 3.33 patients with earplugs, and rounding down would underestimate how many you need.
  • NNT depends on baseline risk. Same RR can give wildly different NNT depending on how high the baseline risk is. The high-risk population gets much more from the same intervention in absolute terms.
  • NNT is the bedside number. RR and OR are great for understanding magnitude. ARR is great for absolute counts. But NNT is the number you actually quote to a patient: "Fit 4 people with earplugs, prevent 1 hearing loss case."
