Step 2 · Mock journal article
Custom musician earplugs and 12-month risk of measurable hearing loss in young adults working at high-volume music venues
Abstract — Background

Young adults regularly exposed to high-volume amplified music at work face elevated risk of noise-induced hearing loss. Custom-molded musician earplugs provide tunable attenuation while preserving sound fidelity, but evidence on long-term protective effect in real-world venues remains limited.

Methods

We conducted a 12-month prospective controlled study of 200 young adults (aged 21–35) employed at amplified-music venues across three metro areas. Patients were group-allocated to either custom-fitted musician earplugs (used during all shifts, n = 100) or standard practice with no formal hearing protection (n = 100). The primary outcome was a measurable threshold shift of ≥15 dB at one or more frequencies between 2–8 kHz on pure-tone audiometry at 12-month follow-up, conducted by a blinded audiologist.

Results

At 12 months, 25 of 100 patients (25%) in the earplug group developed measurable hearing loss, compared with 55 of 100 patients (55%) in the no-protection group. Compared with no protection, custom earplug use was associated with a statistically significant reduction in measurable hearing loss (RR = 0.45, 95% CI [0.30, 0.68], p < .001). The absolute reduction in 12-month risk was 30 percentage points (ARR = 30%, 95% CI [18%, 42%]), corresponding to NNT = 4 (95% CI [3, 6]). The odds of developing measurable hearing loss were substantially lower in the earplug group (OR = 0.27, 95% CI [0.15, 0.50], p < .001).

Conclusions

In this 12-month controlled study, consistent use of custom musician earplugs was associated with a clinically and statistically significant reduction in measurable hearing loss among young adults working at high-volume music venues. Findings support fitting and recommendation as a routine component of audiological practice for at-risk occupational groups.
